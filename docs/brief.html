Proteus: MCMC-Based Autonomous Protein Design

Aly Dhedhi Version 1.0 FOR RESEARCH USE ONLY

Overview

Proteus is an autonomous protein design platform that uses Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) with parallel tempering to optimize peptide sequences against multi-objective biophysical energy functions. The system runs a 3-round iterative design cycle in under 2 seconds on CPU hardware, producing ranked candidate sequences with full biophysical characterization and per-residue mutation rationale.

This brief describes the algorithmic core, energy function, scoring metrics, benchmarking methodology, and competitive differentiation.

1. MCMC Engine

1.1 Parallel Tempering

Proteus runs N = 5 independent Markov chains simultaneously at temperatures T = {0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0}. Low-temperature chains fine-tune local optima; high-temperature chains escape energy barriers to explore novel sequence regions. Replica exchange swaps between adjacent chains are proposed every 50 steps using the Metropolis-Hastings criterion:

P(swap i ↔ j) = exp(min(0, (E_i − E_j)(β_i − β_j)))
where β = 1/T

1.2 Metropolis-Hastings Acceptance

At each step, a mutation proposal is generated by one of four operators (see Section 2). The proposed sequence energy E' is computed and accepted with probability:

P(accept) = exp(min(0, (E_current − E_proposed) / T))

This allows uphill moves (energy-increasing mutations) with temperature-scaled probability, preventing convergence to local minima.

1.3 Convergence Diagnostics

Two convergence metrics are computed after each run:

  • Gelman-Rubin R-hat: measures between-chain to within-chain variance ratio. R-hat < 1.05 indicates the chains have converged to the same posterior distribution. Computed on the last N/2 energy traces per chain.
  • Effective Sample Size (ESS): autocorrelation-adjusted sample count across all chains. ESS > 100 required for statistical validity. Computed via integrated autocorrelation time.

2. Mutation Proposal Operators

OperatorDescriptionSampling weight
Point substitutionReplace one residue; probability proportional to BLOSUM62 row + ESM-2 pseudo-likelihood~60%
Single insertionInsert residue at random position; penalized for excessive length~15%
Single deletionRemove one residue; penalized for sequences shorter than 6 AA~15%
Block swapShuffle a 3–6 residue contiguous segment to a different position~10%

ESM-2 pseudo-likelihood scores (cached from the facebook/esm2_t6_8M_UR50D model) bias point substitutions toward evolutionarily plausible residue identities given local context.

3. Energy Function

The composite energy E(seq) is a weighted sum of six components:

E = 0.50 × (1 − binding)
+ 0.25 × (1 − stability)
+ 0.15 × (1 − solubility)
+ 0.10 × (1 − BBB score)
+ 0.08 × aggregation penalty
+ 0.02 × charge penalty
ComponentImplementation
Binding scoreShannon information content of amino acid frequencies vs. reference natural protein distribution; pocket residue bonus if binding site is specified
Stability scoreSecondary structure propensity: penalizes Pro (helix-breaking) and Gly (flexible), rewards charged residue pairs for salt bridges
Solubility scoreGRAVY hydrophobicity score (Kyte-Doolittle) inverted; + charged residue fraction bonus
BBB scoreSmall residue fraction + charge constraint + aromatic content; relevant for CNS targets
Aggregation penaltyCount of hydrophobic stretches ≥ 3 consecutive residues with GRAVY > 2.0
Charge penaltyNet charge outside ideal range [−3, +3]

Weights are user-adjustable. Design constraints parsed from free-text (e.g. "no cysteines", "high solubility", "thermostable") automatically rebalance the objective.

Each candidate is scored on eight metrics beyond the MCMC energy function:

ΔΔG estimatekcal/molStability score + hydrophobic core heuristic + salt bridge proxy. Negative = stabilizing. Not Rosetta-grade; calibrated to typical globular protein ranges (−2 to −6 kcal/mol).
Aggregation propensity0–1Normalized hydrophobic stretch count (stretches of ≥ 3 residues with GRAVY > 2.0).
Immunogenicity score0–1MHC anchor heuristic: basic residue density (KR) + aromatic anchor frequency (FWY) + length penalty for sequences > 50 AA.
Manufacturability0–1Solubility + cysteine count penalty + length penalty + repetitive region penalty. Proxy for E. coli / CHO recombinant expression feasibility.
Novelty score0–1Shannon entropy of amino acid composition divided by log₂(20). Higher = more diverse residue usage.
Net chargeeSum of CHARGE_SCALE values at physiological pH approximation.
GRAVYrealKyte-Doolittle grand average hydrophobicity; < −0.5 = hydrophilic surface protein.

5. Per-Mutation Rationale

For each accepted mutation in the best candidate, Proteus reports:

  • Chemical property class change: e.g. "nonpolar aliphatic → negative charged"
  • Hydrophobicity shift: e.g. "hydrophobic → hydrophilic"
  • BLOSUM62 score: evolutionary conservation (positive = conservative substitution)
  • Energy delta: signed change in composite energy at acceptance

6. Benchmark Methodology

Proteus is benchmarked on 10 protein targets across 4 disease areas. Binding affinity values are reported in nM (estimated IC50 equivalents).

Proteus values are estimated by calibrating the multi-objective energy score against published IC50 values for known binders on the same targets. The calibration maps the energy score distribution to a nM range consistent with literature values for weak (1000+ nM) to strong (< 10 nM) binders.

AlphaFold values are estimated by running the same energy oracle on the AlphaFold-predicted structure without MCMC optimization. This represents a "structure-only" baseline — AlphaFold does not perform binding optimization.

Published drug values are sourced from peer-reviewed literature via PubMed and DrugBank. All values are computational predictions. No wet-lab validation has been performed on Proteus candidates.

7. Limitations and Honest Scope

  • No structure prediction: Proteus designs sequences, not 3D structures. The pLDDT and ΔΔG estimates are sequence-composition heuristics, not AlphaFold or Rosetta calculations.
  • No docking simulation: Binding affinity estimates are derived from an energy oracle calibrated to literature IC50 ranges, not molecular docking (AutoDock, Glide, etc.).
  • No wet-lab validation: All values are computational predictions. Proteus candidates require synthesis, expression, and experimental binding assays before any biological conclusions.
  • Seed dependence: MCMC output quality depends on the seed sequence and the binding site specification. Incorrect pocket residue definitions will degrade results.
  • Small sequences: The current energy oracle is most accurate for peptides of 6–200 amino acids. Very large proteins (>500 AA) are not well-characterized by the current scoring functions.

9. Stack

  • Backend: FastAPI (Python 3.9), PostgreSQL, Redis, JWT + RBAC auth
  • MCMC core: NumPy, from-scratch Metropolis-Hastings + parallel tempering
  • Embeddings: ESM-2 (facebook/esm2_t6_8M_UR50D) via HuggingFace, cached
  • Frontend: React + TypeScript, Recharts, RCSB 3D viewer
  • Website: Static HTML, no framework dependency
  • Deploy: GitHub Pages (website); self-hosted Docker (backend + frontend)
